# Engineering Log: The Value of Quantifying Development Velocity

As a CTO and engineer with over two decades in the trenches—spanning the evolution of the web to the complexities of Web3, DID, and AI—I've learned that the most dangerous phase of a project is the one where "progress" is felt but not measured. 

Today's work was a seemingly simple chore: updating the dashboard data for my personal engineering hub (`jsong1230.github.io`). While a JSON update might look trivial in a git diff, it represents a critical habit in high-performance engineering: **the quantification of velocity.**

### The Technical Context: Data-Driven Self-Reflection

The change involves updating `src/data/dashboard.json`, which serves as the backend for my activity visualization. By tracking commit frequency, branch activity, and the nature of the last messages across various repositories, I can identify where my cognitive load is being spent.

Looking at the diff, we see a shift in focus:
- **`heritageradio`**: Updated with a new feature regarding API key functionality (`X-API-Key`).
- **`company-page`**: Significant activity increase, with `commits_7d` jumping from 32 to 44.
- **`jsong1230.github.io`**: Transitioning from weekly recaps to a more granular "Auto-generate daily posts" system.

```json
- "last_msg": "Auto-generate weekly recap",
+ "last_msg": "Auto-generate daily posts",
```

### Technical Decisions & Insights

The decision to automate this dashboard—rather than manually logging hours—is a reflection of my philosophy on "Developer Experience" (DX). In my years leading teams at **CPLABS**, I've always advocated for removing friction. If a developer has to spend 30 minutes a day manually reporting what they did, that is 30 minutes of deep work lost.

By treating my own development activity as a data stream, I can apply the same observability principles we use in production environments to my own productivity. 

**Key insights from today's data shift:**
1. **Context Switching Costs:** The activity spikes in `company-page` and `heritageradio` simultaneously indicate a period of high context switching. As an engineer, I know this is where bugs often creep in.
2. **Automation Iteration:** The change from "weekly" to "daily" post generation shows a move toward tighter feedback loops. In AI and Web3 development, the pace of change is so rapid that a weekly cadence is often too slow to capture the nuance of a technical pivot.

### Closing Thoughts

Many engineers view "chore" commits or data updates as noise. However, from a leadership perspective, these are the signals. Whether I am architecting a decentralized identity system or refining a corporate landing page for **CPLABS**, the ability to step back and look at the "diff" of my time is what allows for strategic adjustment.

The goal isn't just to write more code—it's to ensure that the code being written aligns with the highest priority objectives. Today's update is a reminder that the best way to manage a project is to first manage the data surrounding it.
