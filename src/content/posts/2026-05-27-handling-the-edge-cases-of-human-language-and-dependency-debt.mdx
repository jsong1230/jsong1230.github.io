# Handling the "Edge Cases" of Human Language and Dependency Debt

As an engineer, I’ve always believed that the most challenging part of software development isn't the logic itself, but the "messiness" of real-world data. Today was a perfect example of this. Most of my work revolved around refining how we extract meaning from human-entered titles and managing the silent erosion of security in our dependency trees.

## 1. The Battle with Regex: When "ep01" isn't enough

In the `heritageradio` project, I encountered a classic data extraction problem. We have a system that tracks series episodes based on titles. Initially, I relied on a simple regex anchor (`^ep\d+`) to identify episode numbers. It worked for 90% of the cases, but the remaining 10%—the "anomaly zone"—was causing silent data loss.

I discovered three specific failure points:
1. **Positioning:** Some titles ended with `— ep06` instead of starting with it. The `^` anchor was blocking these.
2. **Localization:** In the `YASA_STORY` program, episodes were written in Korean numerals (e.g., "제일화", "제십오화"). A standard `\d+` regex is blind to these.
3. **Data Corruption:** Some master titles were incorrectly parsed from script frontmatter (e.g., "Opening — approx 25s"), requiring manual restoration to the correct era titles.

## 2. Managing Dependency Debt in `meta-info-api`

While the regex work was about logic, the work on `meta-info-api` was about hygiene. Running `npm audit fix` revealed several high and low vulnerabilities across transitive dependencies like `express`, `node-fetch`, and `qs`.

The technical decision here was a balance between security and stability. I updated the `package-lock.json` to resolve these vulnerabilities within the semver range. However, I intentionally held off on downgrading `request-id` to fix a moderate vulnerability because it would have introduced a breaking change. After analyzing the attack vector (specifically the `uuid` buffer argument), I determined that our current implementation doesn't trigger the vulnerability.

**The Insight:** Security updates aren't always "click and forget." As a CTO, I prioritize a "risk-based approach." If a fix breaks the build but the vulnerability is unreachable in our specific execution path, a documented deferral is often safer than a blind update.

## Closing Thoughts

Today’s work reminds me that whether it's a 20-year-old legacy system or a modern Web3 stack at **CPLABS**, the core of engineering remains the same: **paying attention to the edges.** Whether it's a Korean character in a title or a transitive dependency in a lockfile, the quality of the system is defined by how we handle the anomalies.
