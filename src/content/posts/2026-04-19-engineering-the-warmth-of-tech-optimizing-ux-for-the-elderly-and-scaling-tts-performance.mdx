# Engineering the "Warmth" of Tech: Optimizing UX for the Elderly and Scaling TTS Performance

As an engineer and CTO, I’ve spent over two decades building systems that prioritize scalability and efficiency. However, my recent work on the **"Heritage Radio"** project has reminded me of a crucial engineering truth: **the most sophisticated backend is meaningless if the frontend creates a barrier for the end-user.**

Today's development focused on two contrasting but equally important pillars: the hyper-specific UX constraints for elderly users and the raw performance optimization of the AI audio pipeline.

### 1. Designing for Cognitive and Physical Constraints

The most significant addition today was the `claude_design_brief.md`. When building for seniors—specifically those with dementia—standard "Clean UI" or "Modern Minimalist" trends are often failures. For this demographic, "minimalism" can be mistaken for "emptiness" or "lack of function."

I spent a considerable amount of time codifying UX constraints that are non-negotiable. In the diff, you'll see a strict set of rules:
- **No Pure White (#fff):** To prevent glare and eye fatigue (common in cataract patients), I've mandated a `Cream (#F5E6D0)` palette.
- **Touch Targets $\ge$ 48px:** Accounting for hand tremors.
- **No Gestures/Long-press:** Interaction must be a simple, single touch.

**Insight:** In my years at CPLABS, I learned that accessibility isn't just a "feature" to add at the end; it is the core architecture. When the user's cognitive load is limited, the interface must act as a guide, not a puzzle. By creating a structured brief for our AI design sessions, we ensure that the "warmth" of the 1970s retro aesthetic is backed by rigorous accessibility engineering.

### 2. Performance Tuning: Parallelizing the Voice

On the technical side, I tackled a bottleneck in the TTS (Text-to-Speech) pipeline. The goal was to reduce latency in audio generation without sacrificing quality.

I implemented **"Option D"** for the TTS architecture:
- **Round-Robin Distribution:** Deploying 5 instances of `qwen3tts`.
- **Parallel Chunking:** Processing 5 audio chunks concurrently.

By shifting the `docker-compose.yml` to support this distributed approach, we can significantly decrease the "Time to First Byte" (TTFB) for the audio stream. When dealing with AI-generated content, the perceived latency is the biggest enemy of immersion. Parallelizing the workload across multiple instances allows us to maintain a seamless streaming experience, which is critical for a "radio" application.

### 3. The "Small" Fixes that Matter

Finally, I resolved a frustrating bug in the CI/CD pipeline. A simple `rsync` exclude rule (`--exclude='audio'`) was accidentally dropping the frontend audio route because of a missing leading slash (`/audio`).

It's a classic example of how a single character in a configuration file can break a production deployment. It serves as a reminder that as we scale our infrastructure at CPLABS, the rigor applied to the pipeline is just as important as the rigor applied to the application code.

### Final Thoughts

Today was a balance of **empathy and efficiency**. Whether it's ensuring a 70-year-old user can easily find the "Play" button or ensuring a TTS engine can handle parallel requests without lagging, the goal is the same: removing friction.

Technical depth is not just about using the newest framework; it's about knowing exactly which constraint to apply to solve a human problem.
