# Engineering Log: Balancing Automation and Infrastructure Stability

As a CTO, my day is rarely spent writing a single monolithic feature. Instead, it is often a series of surgical interventions across multiple projects—balancing the high-level architectural vision with the gritty reality of infrastructure maintenance. Today was a prime example of this "context-switching" rhythm.

Looking at today's updates to my personal dashboard data, you can see the footprints of three distinct workstreams: **AI infrastructure monitoring, operational reliability, and CI/CD synchronization.**

### 1. AI Infrastructure: The "Canary" Approach
One of the most critical updates today was in the `mirae` repository:
`feat: AI 캐펙스 조기경보 canary_alert.sh (코어위브·오라클 감시)`

When dealing with high-cost AI compute resources (like CoreWeave or Oracle Cloud), "silent failures" are the most expensive kind of bugs. If a GPU cluster hangs or a quota is hit without an immediate alert, you aren't just losing time—you're losing capital. 

I implemented a **Canary Alert system**. Rather than relying solely on the cloud provider's generic health checks, I've introduced a custom script that monitors specific performance metrics and triggers immediate alerts. In the world of AI Ops, proactive "early warning" systems are the only way to maintain a lean Capex budget while ensuring high availability.

### 2. Operational Reliability: Closing the Feedback Loop
In the `metadium_homepage` project, I focused on the "last mile" of monitoring:
`monitor.sh에 Telegram 알림 추가 (이상 감지 시 push)`

A monitoring script that only logs to a file is a script that is ignored. Throughout my 20 years of engineering, I've learned that **visibility is as important as functionality**. By integrating Telegram push notifications into the EC2 health monitor, I've shifted the operational model from *pull* (checking logs) to *push* (being notified). This reduces the Mean Time to Recovery (MTTR) significantly.

### 3. CI/CD and State Sync: The "Dev-Ahead" Dilemma
Finally, in the `heritageradio` project, I tackled a common synchronization headache:
`briefing: dev-ahead 판단 — backend 미배포 + api_keys sync exclude`

One of the trickiest parts of managing a pipeline is the "dev-ahead" state—where the development branch has evolved beyond what is currently deployed in production. The technical decision here was to explicitly exclude `api_keys` from the synchronization process. 

Mixing environment-specific secrets with version-controlled code is a cardinal sin of DevOps. By refining the sync logic to exclude these keys, we ensure that moving code between environments doesn't accidentally overwrite production secrets with development values.

### Final Insights: The Art of the Dashboard
Updating my `dashboard.json` isn't just about bookkeeping; it's about visualizing velocity. When I see the `commits_7d` and `commits_30d` metrics climb across different repos—from AI agents to CPLABS infrastructure—it confirms that the automation layer is working.

The goal of a senior engineer or CTO isn't to write the most code, but to build the systems that make the code reliable, visible, and scalable. Today was about tightening those screws.
