# Weekly Retrospective: Bridging the Gap Between AI Ambition and Production Reality
**Date Range:** April 26, 2026 – May 3, 2026
**Author:** Jeffrey Joo-Han Song

As we approach the May 11th launch date for the **Heritage Radio** project, this past week has been a masterclass in the "final mile" of engineering. While the early stages of a project are often defined by high-level architectural abstractions—Web3, DID, and AI model selection—the closing stages are defined by the relentless pursuit of precision. 

This week, my focus shifted from building the engine to tuning the instrument, ensuring that the intersection of AI-generated content and human experience is seamless, legal, and stable.

### Technical Workstreams & Execution

The bulk of my effort was dedicated to the **Heritage Radio** ecosystem, specifically focusing on the transition from a functional prototype to a production-ready service.

**1. Infrastructure & Deployment**
I finalized the production environment using the AWS CDK, employing a four-stage rollout to contain the "blast radius" of potential failures. A key architectural decision this week was the implementation of an **ALB (Application Load Balancer) Direct** approach for the admin panel. By bypassing CloudFront for internal administrative tools, I was able to balance the immediate need for production agility—managing push notifications and auditing logs—with the necessary security boundaries.

**2. The AI Content Pipeline: Precision vs. Automation**
One of the most challenging aspects of integrating AI into a broadcasting format is the "hard constraint" of time. I spent significant time resolving discrepancies in audio length. We encountered a "10-minute wall" where AI-generated narration exceeded our scheduling slots. The technical challenge here wasn't just trimming audio, but doing so without sacrificing the emotional integrity of the content (e.g., preserving the "farewell" remarks). This required a shift from blanket automation to a more nuanced, policy-driven quality control process, ensuring character counts remained strictly between 4,000 and 4,400 to hit our 7-8 minute target.

**3. Edge Case Resolution & Logic Refinement**
As we neared the D-Day launch, "invisible" bugs began to surface. I addressed several critical edge cases, including:
*   **The Midnight Gap:** Solving KST offset issues that resulted in "negative time" paradoxes (e.g., `-1:30~00:00`) in the UI.
*   **Mapping Mismatches:** Removing "too helpful" server-side logic that was forcing preview content over actual production data, which had caused episode repetition for users.

### Key Technical Insights

The overarching lesson of the week is that **automation without constraints is a liability.** Whether it is the `booksummary` pipeline or the Heritage Radio audio masters, the goal of a CTO is to reduce cognitive load through automation, but that automation must be bounded by strict business and technical guardrails.

Furthermore, this week highlighted the importance of **Engineering Empathy**. When building for the elderly, a UI glitch or a jarring cut in an AI voice isn't just a bug—it's a disruption of a therapeutic experience. The technical precision applied to the audio trimming and time-formatting was, in essence, an exercise in maintaining the dignity and comfort of the end-user.

### Looking Ahead

With the infrastructure now `CREATE_COMPLETE` and the edge cases largely mitigated, the focus for the coming week shifts toward final stress testing and stability. 

As we move toward the May 11th launch, I will be monitoring the interaction between our AI voice synthesis and the production load. My primary goal is to ensure that the "Radio Time Machine" doesn't just function technically, but evokes the precise nostalgia intended for our users. The transition from "Engineer" to "Curator" is now complete; we are no longer just writing code—we are polishing an experience.
