# Bridging Eras: Automating Voice Reference Extraction and Content Scaling

As a CTO, I’ve always believed that the intersection of legacy data and modern AI is where the most interesting engineering challenges lie. Today’s work on the `heritageradio` project was a perfect example of this. We aren't just archiving history; we are building a technical pipeline to make that history "speak" again.

Today was focused on two primary pillars: **Automated Voice Reference (voice_ref) Extraction** and **Mass Content Scaling**.

### 1. The Technical Challenge: From Raw Audio to AI-Ready References

The goal was to extract high-quality voice references from KTV’s "대한뉴스" (Korean News on Film) archives to serve as a baseline for TTS and voice synthesis. The problem is that raw archive files are monolithic; we needed specific, clean segments that represent different eras (70s, 80s, 90s).

I developed a Python-based extraction pipeline (`extract_from_raw.py`) that automates the entire journey from a raw MP3 to a metadata-rich voice reference.

**The Technical Workflow:**
1.  **Targeted Extraction:** Instead of random sampling, I defined specific candidates across three eras (75, 85, 95) and applied multiple offsets (30s, 60s, 90s) to ensure we captured diverse vocal tones within a single broadcast.
2.  **Audio Normalization:** To ensure the AI doesn't learn "noise" or inconsistent volumes, I integrated `ffmpeg` with the `loudnorm` filter:
    ```bash
    -af "loudnorm=I=-20:LRA=7:TP=-2"
    ```
    This is a critical step. In my experience at **CPLABS**, dealing with inconsistent data formats is the biggest bottleneck in AI pipelines. Normalizing the Integrated Loudness (I) to -20 LUFS ensures the voice references are consistent regardless of the original recording quality.
3.  **Automated Transcription:** I integrated OpenAI's Whisper (base model) directly into the script to generate `.meta.json` sidecar files. This transforms a "blind" audio file into a searchable, attributed asset.

**The Result:** Out of 90 attempted segments, 53 were successfully processed and registered, meeting our goal of 10+ active references per era.

### 2. Content Scaling: The "Born Today" and "Joseon Story" Pipelines

Engineering isn't just about the "plumbing" (the code); it's also about the "water" (the content). Today, I pushed a massive amount of structured content:
- **Born Today:** 25 new scripts across 5 different decades.
- **Joseon Story:** 15 new scripts.

To handle this volume, I didn't just manually upload files. I wrote a dedicated upload tool (`upload_born_today.py`). When you reach a certain scale of content, the "manual upload" phase becomes a technical debt. Automating the ingestion of Markdown files into the backend ensures that the data structure remains consistent and reduces the risk of human error during deployment.

### 3. Infrastructure Quick-Fix: Unblocking the Admin

A small but vital change today was removing the `:ro` (read-only) flag from the backend audio volume in `docker-compose.yml`. 

In a production environment, read-only mounts are a best practice for security. However, during the active development of an admin tool—where the system needs to write processed audio or temporary cache files—this becomes a "P0 blocker." I made the conscious decision to open the permissions to unblock the admin workflow, with the plan to implement a more granular `/tmp` write-permission strategy later.

### Final Insights

Today’s work reinforces a core principle I’ve followed for 20 years: **Automate the boring stuff so you can focus on the hard stuff.** 

By automating the audio extraction and content upload, I shifted my focus from "how do I move this file?" to "is the audio quality sufficient for our AI model?" That is where the real value is created.

Moving forward, the next phase will be collecting copyrighted broadcast voices (professional announcers), which will require a more nuanced approach to licensing and attribution.
