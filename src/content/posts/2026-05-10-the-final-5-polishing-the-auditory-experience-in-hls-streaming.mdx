# Refining the Auditory Experience: From Crossfade Tuning to TTS Strategy

As an engineer, I’ve always believed that the difference between a "working" product and a "polished" product lies in the final 5% of the details. In the world of audio streaming and HLS (HTTP Live Streaming), those details aren't just about the code—they are about the human perception of sound.

Today's work on the `heritageradio` project was a deep dive into exactly that: the intersection of technical implementation and auditory UX.

### 1. Solving the "Rough Transition" Problem

We received feedback that certain transitions—specifically from voice narration to background "tail" music—felt jarring. In audio engineering, a transition that is too abrupt can break the listener's immersion, especially in a "heritage" or "storytelling" context where a seamless flow is critical.

The technical fix was a precise adjustment of the crossfade durations. I increased the `_XFADE_DUR_BY_PAIR` for `("voice", "tail")` and `("voice", "music")` from **1.5 seconds to 3.0 seconds**.

```python
# Before: 1.5s (felt rough)
# After: 3.0s (smoother, more natural transition)
_XFADE_DUR_BY_PAIR = {
    ...
    ("voice", "tail"): 3.0,
    ("voice", "music"): 3.0,
    ...
}
```

While a 1.5-second difference seems negligible in a codebase, it is a massive shift in perceived audio quality. By doubling the fade time, we allow the voice to bleed more naturally into the music, creating a professional "radio-style" blend.

### 2. Eliminating Trailing Silence in Music Slots

Another critical issue was the presence of unexpected silence at the end of music slots (e.g., `OLDIES_POP` and `GAYO_OLD_2`). This usually happens when the accumulated duration of the music tracks is shorter than the allocated slot duration.

To solve this, I implemented a "tail-music chain" logic for music slots, mirroring the policy we already had for voice slots. If the `target_tail` (the gap between current content and slot duration) exceeds a minimum threshold, the system now fetches and appends additional "tail" tracks to fill the void.

```python
content_sec = sum(_ffprobe_dur(p["path"]) for p in pieces)
target_tail = slot_dur - content_sec
if target_tail >= MIN_TAIL_TARGET and ADMIN_TOKEN:

```

This ensures that the stream remains "alive" until the very last millisecond of the slot, preventing those awkward gaps that make a professional stream feel like an amateur playlist.

### 3. Strategic Shift in TTS Engines

On the frontend/admin side, we made a strategic decision regarding the `BEAUTIFUL_KOREA` program. We shifted its TTS engine from `qwen3tts` (automated) to `typecast` (external synthesis + manual upload).

In my experience at **CPLABS**, I've learned that automation is great for scale, but high-quality content often requires a "human-in-the-loop" approach. For specific programs that demand a certain emotional resonance or precise inflection, the flexibility of an external tool like Typecast—followed by a manual admin upload—outperforms a fully automated pipeline.

### Final Thoughts

Today's changes highlight a recurring theme in my 20-year career: **The importance of the feedback loop.** Whether it's a director's feedback on a "rough transition" or a user's preference for a specific voice tone, the best technical decisions are those informed by the actual experience of the end-user.

Engineering isn't just about making the system run; it's about making the system *feel* right.
