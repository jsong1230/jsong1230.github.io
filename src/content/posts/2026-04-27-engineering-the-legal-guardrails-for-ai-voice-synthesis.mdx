# Engineering the Legal Guardrails for AI Voice Synthesis

As a CTO, my role often oscillates between deep-diving into kernel-level optimizations and navigating the complex intersection of law and technology. Today’s work was a prime example of the latter. While the git diffs might look like simple documentation updates, the technical and strategic decisions behind them are critical for the long-term viability of our AI services.

## The Problem: The "Grey Area" of Public Domain AI Training

We are currently developing **'Radio Time Machine'**, an AI-driven digital reminiscence therapy service for seniors. A core technical requirement is recreating the specific vocal atmosphere of announcers and DJs from the 1950s to the 1990s to evoke nostalgia and cognitive stimulation.

We identified a high-quality dataset from KTV (Korea TV), released under the "KOGL Type 1" (Public Domain) license. On the surface, this seems like a green light. However, as an engineer with two decades of experience—including my time at **CPLABS**—I’ve learned that "Open Data" does not always mean "Open for AI Training."

The core problem is the distinction between **Copyright** (the recording/broadcast) and **Neighboring Rights (Performers' Rights)**. Even if the broadcast itself is public domain, the specific vocal characteristics and the "performance" of the announcer may still be protected under Article 2, Paragraph 4 of the Copyright Act.

## Technical and Strategic Decisions

Today, I drafted a formal inquiry to KTV to secure a "legal safe harbor." The decision to be radically transparent in this communication was intentional.

In the diff, you'll notice a specific note:
> *"AI 음성합성 참조 데이터" 표현을 명시 — 단순 영상 인용이 아닌 voice clone 용도임을 숨기지 않음.*
> *(Explicitly stating "AI voice synthesis reference data" — not hiding that this is for voice cloning, not simple citation.)*

Many teams make the mistake of using vague language like "research purposes" or "data analysis" to avoid friction. However, from a risk management perspective, a vague approval is useless. If we are to build a commercial service with an ad-revenue model, we need an explicit confirmation that covers:
1. **Voice Cloning:** Using the audio as a reference for TTS (Text-to-Speech) models.
2. **Commercialization:** Ensuring that the "Public Domain" status extends to commercial app distribution.
3. **Neighboring Rights:** Confirming that the performers' rights have been waived or are managed by the agency.

## Insights from the Process

This task highlights a shift in the modern CTO's responsibility. In the Web2 era, "solving" a problem usually meant optimizing a query or scaling a cluster. In the Web3 and AI era, solving a problem often means **defining the legal boundary of the data pipeline.**

If we proceed without this explicit confirmation, we risk a "poisoned dataset" scenario where a legal challenge forces us to delete our trained weights—a catastrophic technical debt that no amount of refactoring can fix.
